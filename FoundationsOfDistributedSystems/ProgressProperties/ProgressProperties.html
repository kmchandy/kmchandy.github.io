Progress Properties:
Key Properties of Leads-To \(\leadsto\)

This module defines three key properties of \(\leadsto\). The definitions are in terms of sets of vertices and fair paths in labeled graphs. We use these properties frequently in designing distributed algorithms. This module also has examples of the use of induction to prove progress properties.

We denote the complement of a set \(P\) by \(P^{c}\).

Transient and Transitory Vertex Sets

A vertex set \(P\) is defined to be transient exactly when there exists a fair label \(a\) such that all edges labeled \(a\) that start at a vertex in \(P\) terminate at a vertex in \(P^{c}\).

\( transient(P) \quad \equiv \quad \exists \; \textrm{fair} \; a: (v \in P) \wedge (v \stackrel{a}{\rightarrow} w) \; \Rightarrow \; w \in P^{c} \)

A vertex set \(P\) is defined to be transitory exactly when \(P \leadsto P^{c}\).

\( transitory(P) \quad \equiv \quad P \leadsto P^{c} \)

Atomic Property of \(\leadsto\)

\( transient(P) \Rightarrow transitory(P) \)

Proof outline: A fair path cannot remain forever in \(P\) because the path traverses all fair labels, at least one of which takes the path out of \(P\).

We call this property the atomic property because it is based on a single label that is executed infinitely often.

\(P\) is transitory means that all fair paths that visit \(P\) then leave \(P\) after a finite number of hops. If \(P\) is transient then \(P\) is transitory. The converse, however, is not necessarily true. Transience requires the existence of a single fair label from every vertex in \(P\) to vertices outside \(P\). By contrast, transitoriness merely requires that fair paths leave \(P\), possibly through multiple steps.

Basic Property of \(\leadsto\)

\( (P \rightarrow (P \cup Q)) \wedge transitory(P) \quad \Rightarrow \quad (P \leadsto Q) \)

Proof outline: \(P \; \rightarrow \; (P \cup Q) \) implies that every path from a vertex in \(P\) either (1) remains in \(P\) forever or (2) visits a vertex in \(Q\). But, \(transitory(P)\) implies that the first case is not possible for fair paths.

We call this property the basic property because it is the property on which most proofs of progress are based.

Transitivity of \(\leadsto\)

\( (P \leadsto Q) \wedge (Q \leadsto R) \; \Rightarrow \; (P \leadsto R) \)

The proof is straightforward.

The atomic, basic and transitivity property are the fundamental properties of \(\leadsto\).

Example

We use the following example graph from the previous module. Recall that in the example, each vertex has exactly one outgoing edge with a given label. Edges from a vertex back to itself are not shown in the diagram.

Example of the Atomic Property

Every edge labeled \(b\) from a vertex in the set \{8, 9\} terminates at a vertex outside the set. Because \(b\) is a fair label:

\( \{8, 9\} \leadsto \{8, 9\}^{c} \)

Example of the Basic Property

We showed that

\( \{8, 9\} \leadsto \{8, 9\}^{c} \)

From the graph:

\( \{8, 9\} \rightarrow \{1, 2,8, 9\} \)

Therefore:

\( \{8, 9\} \leadsto \{1, 2\} \)

Example of the Transitivity of \(\leadsto\)

From the atomic property and the infinitely-often execution of label \(a\):

\( \{10\} \leadsto \{8, 9\} \)

We have shown that:

\( \{8, 9\} \leadsto \{1, 2\} \)

Therefore:

\( \{10\} \leadsto \{1, 2\} \)

Summary

We use the atomic, basic, and transitivity to prove progress of most of the systems studied in this course.
