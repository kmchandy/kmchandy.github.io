Properties of \(\rightarrow\) and \(\leadsto\)

This module presents results about \(\rightarrow\) and \(\leadsto\) which are defined in the previous chapter Concepts in Temporal Logic. The module presents results on transitivity and the infinitely-often property which allows us to deduce properties about \(\leadsto\). The module also has a list of observations about \(\rightarrow\) and \(\leadsto\); these observations will be useful in proving the correctness of algorithms.

Transitivity

\( (X \leadsto Y) \wedge (Y \leadsto Z) \; \Rightarrow \; (X \leadsto Z) \)

Proof outline.

From the left-hand side, for every path \(p\):

\( p_{0} \in X \; \Rightarrow \; (\exists i: p_{i} \in Y) \; \Rightarrow \; (\exists j \geq i: p_{j} \in Z) \)

Infinitely Often Property

The complement of a set \(X\) is denoted by \(X^{c}\).

\( (X \rightarrow (X \cup Y)) \wedge (X \leadsto X^{c}) \quad \Rightarrow \quad (X \leadsto Y) \)

Proof: \(X \; \rightarrow \; (X \cup Y) \) implies that every fair path from a vertex in \(X\) either (1) remains in \(X\) forever or (2) visits a vertex in \(Y\). But, \(X \leadsto X^{c}\) implies that the first case is not possible.

Next is a list of useful observations.

Observations

The proofs of these observations follow directly from the definitions given in the previous chapter.

Monotonicity

\( P \subseteq Q \quad \Rightarrow \quad N(P) \subseteq N(Q) \)

\( P \subseteq Q \quad \Rightarrow \quad A(P) \subseteq A(Q) \)

Distributes over Conjunction

\( N(X \cap Y) = N(X) \cap N(Y) \)

\( A(X \cap Y) = A(X) \cap A(Y) \)

Relations and Functions

\( X \rightarrow Y \quad \equiv \quad X \subseteq N(Y) \)

\( X \stackrel{*}{\rightarrow} Y \quad \equiv \quad X \subseteq A(Y) \)

Intersections and Unions of Stable Sets are Stable

\( \textit{stable}(X) \wedge \textit{stable}(Y) \; \Rightarrow \textit{stable}(X \cap Y) \)

\( \textit{stable}(X) \wedge \textit{stable}(Y) \; \Rightarrow \textit{stable}(X \cup Y) \)

Corollary

The stable sets that are subsets of \(X\) form a lattice with the empty set at the bottom and \(A(X)\) at the top.

Inclusion

\( (X \subseteq Y) \; \Rightarrow \; (X \leadsto Y) \)

Strengthen Left Side

\( (X \subseteq Y) \wedge (Y \leadsto Z) \; \Rightarrow \; (X \leadsto Z) \)

\( (X \subseteq Y) \wedge (Y \rightarrow Z) \; \Rightarrow \; (X \rightarrow Z) \)

Weaken Right Side

\( (X \leadsto Y) \wedge (Y \subseteq Z) \; \Rightarrow \; (X \leadsto Z) \)

\( (X \rightarrow Y) \wedge (Y \subseteq Z) \; \Rightarrow \; (X \rightarrow Z) \)

Union Left Side

\( (X \leadsto Z) \wedge (Y \leadsto Z) \quad \equiv \quad (X \cup Y) \leadsto Z \)

\( (X \rightarrow Z) \wedge (Y \rightarrow Z) \quad \equiv \quad (X \cup Y) \rightarrow Z \)

Intersection Right Side

\( (X \rightarrow Y) \wedge (X \rightarrow Z) \quad \equiv \quad X \rightarrow (Y \cap Z) \)

Intersection Right Side of Stable Sets

For stable \(X\) and stable\(Y\):

\( (X \leadsto Y) \wedge (X \leadsto Z) \quad \equiv \quad X \rightarrow (Y \cap Z) \)

Summary

We use \(\leadsto\) as the central concept to reason about safety. We use eventually to specify systems. These are the only concepts that we use to prove that systems are safe. Transitivity of \(\leadsto\) and the infinitely-often property are used to reason about almost every algorithm in this course.
