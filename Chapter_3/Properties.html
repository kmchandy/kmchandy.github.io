Key Properties of Leads-To \(\leadsto\)

This chapter has two main modules: the first gives three properties of \(\leadsto\) that we use to prove progress for every system studied in this course. The chapter also has a module on the composition of the functions next, always, and eventually, and the relations \(\rightarrow\), \(\stackrel{*}{\rightarrow}\), and \(\leadsto\). In addition, the chapter has supporting modules with diagrams and self tests.

Next, we describe the fundamental property that we use to prove progress. We denote the complement of a set \(P\) by \(P^{c}\).

Atomic Property of \(\leadsto\)

If for a set \(P\) of vertices, there exists an infinitely-often label \(a\) such that all edges labeled \(a\) that start at a vertex in \(P\) terminate at a vertex in \(P^{c}\), then:

\( P \leadsto P^{c} \)

Proof outline: A fair path cannot remain forever in \(P\) because the path traverses the edge labeled \(a\) which takes the path out of \(P\).

Example of the Atomic Property

In the graph, because of infinitely-often label \(b\):

\( \{8, 9\} \leadsto \{8, 9\}^{c} \)

We call this property the atomic property because it is based on a single label that is executed infinitely often.

Basic Property of \(\leadsto\)

\( (P \rightarrow (P \cup Q)) \wedge (P \leadsto P^{c}) \quad \Rightarrow \quad (P \leadsto Q) \)

Proof outline: \(P \; \rightarrow \; (P \cup Q) \) implies that every fair path from a vertex in \(P\) either (1) remains in \(P\) forever or (2) visits a vertex in \(Q\). But, \(P \leadsto P^{c}\) implies that the first case is not possible.

Example of the Basic Property

We showed that

\( \{8, 9\} \leadsto \{8, 9\}^{c} \)

From the graph:

\( \{8, 9\} \rightarrow \{1, 2,8, 9\} \)

Therefore:

\( \{8, 9\} \leadsto \{1, 2\} \)

We call this property the basic property because it is the property on which most proofs of progress are based.

Transitivity of \(\leadsto\)

\( (X \leadsto Y) \wedge (Y \leadsto Z) \; \Rightarrow \; (X \leadsto Z) \)

Proof outline.

From \(X \leadsto Y\), for every path \(p\):

\( p_{0} \in X \; \Rightarrow \; (\exists i: p_{i} \in Y) \)

From \(Y \leadsto Z\), for every path \(p\):

\( p_{i} \in Y \; \Rightarrow \; (\exists j \geq i: p_{j} \in Z) \)

Therefore:

\( p_{0} \in X \; \Rightarrow \; (\exists j: p_{j} \in Z) \)

Example of the Transitivity of \(\leadsto\)

From the atomic property and the infinitely-often execution of label \(a\):

\( \{10\} \leadsto \{8, 9\} \)

We have shown that:

\( \{8, 9\} \leadsto \{1, 2\} \)

Therefore:

\( \{10\} \leadsto \{1, 2\} \)

Summary

We use the atomic, basic, and transitivity properties of \(\leadsto\) to prove progress for almost all the systems studied in this course.
