Composition

This module has examples of composition of the functions \(A\), \(E\), \(N\) and the relation \(\leadsto\).

Functional Composition

In the previous chapter we defined the following functions from vertex sets to vertex sets: \(N\), \(A\), and \(E\). We also defined the following binary relations on vertex sets: \(\rightarrow\), \(\stackrel{*}{\rightarrow}\), and \(\leadsto\). Next we give examples of compositions of these functions and relations. We use the following labeled graph for examples in this module. In this graph, the edges with labels \(a\) and \(b\) are traversed infinitely often by all fair paths. Fair paths traverse edges with label \(c\) an arbitrary number of times.

Always Eventually

A vertex \(v\) is in \(A(E(X))\) exactly when all fair paths that start at \(v\) visit \(X\) infinitely often.

Proof Outline

By definition, a vertex \(v\) is in \(A(E(X))\) exactly when every vertex on every path starting at \(v\) is in \(E(X)\).

By definition, a vertex \(w\) is in \(E(X)\) exactly when every fair path starting at \(w\) visits a vertex in \(X\).

So, for a path \(p\) that starts at vertex \(v\) in \(A(E(X))\), there exists some \(i\) such that \(p_{i} \in E(X)\). Since \(p_{i}\) remains in \(A(E(X))\), there exists a \(j > i\) such that \(p_{j} \in E(X)\).

Example of A(E(X))

Compute \(A(E(\{3, 4, 8, 9\}))\)

\( E(\{3, 4, 8, 9\}) = \{0, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10\} \)

\( A(\{0, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10\}) = \{0, 3, 4, 5, 6\} \)

Therefore, \( A(E(\{3, 4, 8, 9\})) = \{0, 3, 4, 5, 6\} \)

Consider fair paths from any vertex in \(A(E(\{3, 4, 8, 9\}))\), for example vertex \(0\). The path visits \(\{3, 4\}\) repeatedly, forever. So, \(\{3, 4\}\) appears infinitely often in the path.

Consider a vertex in \(E(\{3, 4, 8, 9\})\) which is not in \(A(E(\{3, 4, 8, 9\}))\), for example vertex \(10\). All fair paths from \(10\) visit \(\{3, 4\}\), but only once - not infinitely often.

Eventually Always

A vertex \(v\) is in \(E(A(X))\) exactly when all fair paths from \(v\) visit and remain forever in \(X\).

Proof Outline

By definition, a vertex \(v\) is in \(E(A(X))\) exactly when every fair path starting at \(v\) visits a vertex in \(A(X)\).

By definition, a vertex \(w\) is in \(A(X)\) exactly when every vertex on every path starting at \(w\) remains in \(X\).

So, any path \(p\) that starts at vertex \(v\) in \(E(A(X))\), visits a vertex \(w\) in \(A(X)\) and thereafter remains forever in \(X\).

Example of E(A(X))

Compute \(E(A(\{1, 2\}))\).

\( A(\{1, 2\}) = \{1, 2\} \)

\( E(\{1, 2\}) = \{1, 2, 8, 9, 10\} \)

Therefore, \( E(A(\{1, 2\})) = \{1, 2, 8, 9, 10\} \)

Consider a vertex in \(E(A(\{1, 2\}))\), say vertex \(10\). All fair paths from vertex \(10\) visit vertex \(1\) or vertex \(2\), and then remain forever within \(\{1, 2\}\).

More examples of functional composition of \(N\), \(A\), \(E\) can be seen in the module Diagrams and Examples.

Summary

The central point of this module is that \(N\), \(A\), and \(E\) are merely functions from vertex sets to vertex sets, and functional composition of them produce useful concepts. The compositions used more often in specifications are \(A(E())\) and \(E(A())\), while other forms such as \(N(E())\) and \(A(E(A()))\) are used less often.
