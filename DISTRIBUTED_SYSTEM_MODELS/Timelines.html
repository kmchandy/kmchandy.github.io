Past, Future and Cuts in Dataflow

This page introduces the concept of a cut of a dataflow graph and relates a cut to the only notions of time -- past and future -- in dataflow. A cut separates past from future. Cuts of dataflow are central to understanding detection algorithms such as deadlock detection and for global snapshot algorithms that determine states of distributed systems.

Cuts of a Dataflow Graph

The only concept of time in dataflow is that of before and after. Recall that a vertex \(v\) is before a vertex \(w\) in the dataflow graph exactly when there is a path from \(v\) to \(w\). And \(w\) is after \(v\) exactly when \(v\) is before \(w\).

A cut of a dataflow graph is a partition of the set of vertices of the graph into subsets past and future such that all events before past events are past events, or equivalently all events after future events are future events.

A cut of a dataflow graph is an instance of the general cut of a graph, and more specifically a cut of a flow network where the source is an initial vertex and the sink is a final vertex.

The State at a Cut

The state of the system at a cut (past, future) is specified by the labels of edges from past to future.

The top diagram of figure 1 shows a cut in which vertices in past are colored red and vertices in future are green. The curved black line is the boundary separating past from future. In figure 1 all edges to past events are from past events, and all edges from future events are to future events.

Properties of Cuts of Dataflow

The proofs of these properties are straigtforward and are given here.

Dataflows of past events and future events.

Given a cut (past, future):
  1. There exists exists a dataflow consisting only of past events, and there exists exists a dataflow consisting only of future events.
  2. There exists a computation that starts at the initial state of the dataflow, executes the events in past and then executes events in future.
From these two results we get the following result which is used in the global snapshot algorithm.

Computations along Increasing Cuts

Let \(S_{i}\) be the state at a cut (past\(_{i}\) , future\(_{i}\)), for \(i = 0, 1, \ldots\) where:

For all \(i\): past\(_{i}\) \(\subseteq\) past\(_{i+1}\),

There exists a computation from state \(S_{0}\) that visits the states \(S_{i}\) in increasing order of \(i\).

Cuts at Initial Sequences of Agent Steps.

Next, we assume that agents are indexed \(i\) for \(0 \leq i < N\). We restrict attention to a cut (past, future) specified by a vector \(n\) where past consists of the first \(n[k]\) steps of agent \(k\).

For a channel \(c\), let \(c_{sent}\) and \(c_{rcvd}\) be the numbers of messages sent and received along the channel at each point in a computation.

Properties used in Detection Algorithms
There exists a cut (past, future) specified by a vector \(n\), where past consists of the first \(n[k]\) steps of agent \(k\), exactly when:
  1. Every message received in past is sent in past,
  2. which is equivalent to: for all channels \(c\): \(c_{sent} \geq c_{rcvd}\).
