Introduction

These notes describe a collection of algorithms used in concurrent systems. Many algorithms for different problems use similar concepts. Ideas such as divide-and-conquer and dynamic programming help in devising sequential algorithms for a variety of problems. These notes emphasize concepts underlying concurrent algorithms.

An example of a fuzzy, but nevertheless useful, concept is that of an indestructible token. An agent that holds the token "knows" that no other agent holds the same token. Concepts such as knowledge and tokens are anthropomorphic -- they ascribe human attributes to algorithmic constructs. Anthropomorphisms often lead to incorrect algorithms. They can, however, also help us invent solutions. These notes define concepts such as tokens formally, and then show how the same concept is used in a variety of algorithms.

Elementary graph theory is the only prerequisite for understanding this material. State transition systems are represented as labeled directed graphs. System safety and progress are defined in terms of paths in graphs.

The descriptions of algorithms include outlines of proofs of correctness. Enough detail is provided to allow readers to construct complete proofs such as those in Unity and TLA+.

The algorithms in this collection are chosen somewhat arbitarily. More algorithms are being added. The description of each algorithm is self contained and the descriptions do not have to be read sequentially. These notes have been used in an introductory undergraduate course.

K. Mani Chandy, Emeritus Simon Ramo Professor, California Institute of Technology
