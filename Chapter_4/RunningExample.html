A Running Example

This module describes an example that we use to illustrate concepts in the following modules. This module also has an example for self tests.

Finding the Earliest Meeting Time

Three agents - Maya, Liu, and Eve - want to find a day at which all of them can meet. The count of days start at 0, and none of them can meet at day 0. Associated with each agent \(j\) is a function \(f_{j}\) from days to days, where \(f_{j}(d)\) is the earliest day at or after day \(d\) that agent \(j\) can meet. So:

\( f_{j}(d) \geq d \)

and, since agent \(j\) can meet on day \(f_{j}(d)\):

\( f_{j}(f_{j}(d)) = f_{j}(d) \)

For example, assume that Maya, Liu and Eve are agents \(0\), \(1\), and \(2\). Maya, Liu and Eve can only meet on days that are multiples of 2, 3, 5, respectively (but none can meet on day 0). So:

\( f_{0}(5) = 6, \; f_{0}(6) = 6, \; f_{0}(7) = 8 \)
\( f_{1}(5) = 6, \; f_{1}(6) = 6, \; f_{1}(7) = 9 \)
\( f_{2}(5) = 5, \; f_{2}(6) = 10, \; f_{2}(7) = 10 \)

In the example, the earliest day that Maya, Liu and Eve can all meet is the least common multiple of 2, 3, and 5 which is 30.

The Earliest Meeting Time

In general, the function \(f_{j}\) is private to agent \(j\). The designers of the distributed system don't know \(f_{j}\). We will assume, however, that there exists some day at which all agents can meet. Let the earliest such day be \(m\); so:
  1. All agents can meet on day \(m\).

    \( \forall j: f_{j}(m) = m \)

  2. For each earlier day \(t\), at least one agent cannot meet at \(t\).

    \( \forall t < m: \exists j: f_{j}(t) > t \)

Associated with agent \(j\) is a variable \(t_{j}\), where in all states in all trajectories we require:

\( \forall j: t_{j} \leq m \)

The above formula, in vector notation is \(t \leq M\) where \(M\) is a vector all of whose elements are \(m\) and whose length is the number of agents. In our example, \(m\) is 30, and \(M = [30, 30, 30]\).

Initial Condition

Let \(\emptyset\) be a vector of \(N\) zeroes. The initial condition is:

\(t = \emptyset\)

A Safety Property

A safety property is that in all states in all trajectories \(t \leq M\):

\( (t = \emptyset) \; \stackrel{*}{\rightarrow} \; (t \leq M) \)

This formula is equivalent to:

\( (t = \emptyset) \; \Rightarrow \; A(t \leq M) \)

A Progress Property

A progress property is that each agent \(j\) eventually sets its time \(t_{j}\) to \(m\).

\( (t = \emptyset) \; \leadsto \; (t = M) \)

Another Safety Property

After all agents set their times to the earliest meeting time, we want the agents to leave their times unchanged, i.e.

\( stable(t = M) \)

This is because the system has to detect that all agents have come to a consensus, and this detection is easier if the agents don't change their values after they reach consensus.

Summary of the Specification

The variables of the system are \(t_{j}\), all \(j\), and the specifications are:

Safety: \( (t = \emptyset) \; \stackrel{*}{\rightarrow} \; (t \leq M) \)

Stability: \( (t = M) \; \stackrel{*}{\rightarrow} \; (t = M) \)

Progress: \( (t = \emptyset) \; \leadsto \; (t = M) \)
